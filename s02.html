Point Estimators

$$\hat{\mu} = \bar{y}$$ $$\hat{\pi} = p$$
    In other words, the unbiased estimators of population mean and population proportion are sample mean and sample proportion.

Confidence Intervals

  • Basic idea: point estimate plus/minus "z" standard errors.

95% Confidence Interval for Proportion

$$\hat{\pi} \pm 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{\pi}(1-\hat{\pi})}{n}}$$

95% Confidence Interval for Difference in Proportions

$$ (\hat{\pi}_2 - \hat{\pi}_1) \pm 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{\pi}_1(1-\hat{\pi}_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\hat{\pi}_2(1-\hat{\pi}_2)}{n_2}}$$

Qualitative Approaches

Qualitative-Quantitative Divide

  • Words vs. Numbers
  • Inductive vs. Deductive
  • Interpretivist vs. Positivist Epistemology

But...

  • One can incorporate numbers into qualitative analysis (and words into quantitative).
  • Both types usually combine induction and deduction.
  • Both include a wide variety of epistemologies and ontologies.

Qualitative Research Questions

  • Often broader and less explicitly stated than quantitative ones.

    I had research questions vaguely in mind, and I was already making mental comparisons between what I was seeing and what the sociology literature had to say.

    Duneier, Mitchell. 1999. Sidewalk. New York: Straus and Giroux.

    From the beginning of my time as a sociologist I’ve been interested in the struggle of human beings to live in accordance to moral worth. And one of the reasons that Sixth Avenue was a strategic site for me was that the challenges to living a moral life there were greater. Here was a setting where you had people coming out of prison with felony convictions and no ability to get jobs or housing. So the question was how you survive in those circumstances and still struggle to live in accordance to standards of moral worth?
